Analyst Notes: Apple's Automotive Ambitions and the Lidar Landscape
By: Sarah Chen, Senior Tech Analyst, Morgan Stanley Research
Date: March 2026 (Internal Draft — Not for Distribution)

Apple Inc. (NASDAQ: AAPL) has been quietly exploring strategic options
in the autonomous driving space for over a decade. The so-called
"Project Titan" has consumed billions of dollars with limited tangible
output. Sources close to the company suggest that management is finally
shifting strategy from building a full self-driving vehicle to acquiring
proven technology providers.

Apple's cash reserves currently stand at over $60 billion, giving it
ample firepower for mid-sized acquisitions. Last month alone, the
company cut $10 billion in planned capital expenditure from its
automotive division, signaling a pivot away from in-house development.

The autonomous driving sensor market is projected to grow from
$3.5 billion in 2025 to over $15 billion by 2030, representing a
compelling entry point for a company like Apple that needs to catch
up to Waymo and Tesla in the self-driving race.

Several lidar technology companies have been rumored as potential
targets. Luminar Technologies, with a current market cap of roughly
$1.2 billion, has been frequently mentioned in supply chain chatter.
AEye Inc. and Innoviz have also seen their stock prices rally on
acquisition speculation.

However, industry insiders indicate that Apple's interest may be
focused on a smaller, privately-held lidar startup based in Southern
California. This company was valued at approximately $300 million
during its last funding round in 2024 and has developed a unique
solid-state lidar architecture that could be integrated into future
Apple products.

If Apple were to successfully enter and capture even 15% of the
autonomous driving sensor market by 2028, analysts estimate the
combined entity could unlock over $5 billion in annual revenue by
the end of the decade.

A combined Apple-LidarCo entity would also benefit from vertical
integration with Apple's in-house chip design (A-series and M-series
processors), potentially reducing bill-of-materials costs by 20-30%
compared to competitors purchasing off-the-shelf components.

The deal structure remains unclear, but based on precedent transactions
in the autonomous driving space — including Amazon's acquisition of Zoox
and Intel's purchase of Mobileye — the acquisition would likely be
structured as a mix of cash and stock consideration.

We expect further clarity during Apple's next earnings call, where
Tim Cook may address the company's long-term strategy in autonomous
systems. Until then, investors should watch for regulatory filings
that could reveal the contours of any definitive agreement.
